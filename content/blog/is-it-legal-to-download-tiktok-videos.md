This question comes up a lot, and the honest answer is: it depends on what you do with the video afterward, not on the act of downloading itself. Here's a straightforward breakdown — not legal advice, just the practical landscape.

## The short version

Downloading a copy of a video for **personal, private use** — watching it offline, saving something you find funny, keeping a backup of your own content — is low-risk almost everywhere. Where things get legally serious is **republishing** someone else's video, especially if you strip their credit, monetize it, or pass it off as your own.

## Whose video is it?

- **Your own videos**: no issue at all. Downloading your own uploads (say, to edit or repost elsewhere) is completely fine.
- **Videos you have permission to use**: if a creator has explicitly said you can use their clip — for a compilation, a reaction video, a business collaboration — you're covered, ideally with that permission in writing.
- **Someone else's video, no permission**: this is where copyright law applies. The video is the creator's intellectual property the moment they post it, whether or not it has a watermark.

## What TikTok's terms actually say

TikTok's terms of service ask users not to download content unless a download feature is provided, and generally reserve rights to the platform and its creators. Using a third-party downloader technically sits outside that framework. In practice, TikTok has never gone after individuals for personal-use downloads, but it's worth knowing the terms exist, especially if you're building something at scale (e.g., a business that reposts TikTok content).

## Fair use, briefly

Many jurisdictions (the U.S. included) recognize "fair use" or "fair dealing" exceptions for things like commentary, criticism, parody, and news reporting. A short clip used to critique or discuss the original, with attribution, is much more defensible than reposting the whole video for engagement farming. Fair use is evaluated case-by-case, though — there's no fixed rule like "under 10 seconds is always fine."

## A simple rule of thumb

If you'd be comfortable with the original creator seeing exactly how you used their video — and they'd have no reasonable objection — you're probably in safe territory. If you're monetizing, removing credit, or claiming the work as your own, that's the point where legal risk (and just basic fairness to the creator) goes up.

Tools like DLTok are built for the low-risk case: saving something for yourself, quickly, without a watermark getting in the way. What you do with the file afterward is on you.
